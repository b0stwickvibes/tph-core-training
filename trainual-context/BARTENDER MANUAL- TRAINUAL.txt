## 🍹Making Cocktails

---

### ⚖️**Balance in Cocktails**

Before we discuss the specific techniques involved in creating a cocktail, there are two key concepts you must fully understand.

### **Balance**

For our purposes, balance refers to the harmonious contrast of flavors and textures in a cocktail. This is especially important when creating a new drink.

#### **Example: The Margarita**

**Ingredients:** Tequila, triple sec, and lime juice

* *This is one example without agave or simple syrup*

**Flavor Balance:**

* The potential sharpness of the tequila is perfectly balanced by the sweet, orange-flavored triple sec and the tart lime juice.  
* The taste of the tequila is not masked but highlighted by the other ingredients.

When describing this drink, you wouldn't say it is solely sweet or sour. Instead, the flavors are balanced, showcasing the tequila while being complemented by the sweetness of the triple sec and the tartness of the lime juice. This is what we mean by balance.

### 🧊**Ice & Dilution in Cocktails**

Ice, or rather melted ice, is the secret ingredient in every cocktail. Dilution is an essential tool for bartenders to understand and appreciate.

### **Dilution in Cocktails**

#### **Types of Ice:**

* Ice cubes range from huge 2-inch cubes to finely shredded ice.  
* Each type has an appropriate use behind the bar.

#### **Dilution:**

* The bigger the cube, the slower the dilution.  
* Proper dilution can enhance the taste of a fine bourbon.  
* Too much dilution can water down and ruin the drink.

Understanding the role of ice and its impact on dilution is crucial for creating well-balanced and flavorful cocktails.

## 🔧Techniques

---

### ❇️**Standard Ounce Measurements**

It’s essential to memorize these measurements, as different venues may use varying standards. Here are the standard units we follow for all recipes and single shots.

| Standard Unit for All Recipes (Fluid Ounces) |
| :---: |
| **Shot: 1.25 oz** |
| **Single: 1.25 oz** |
| **Double: 2.25 oz** |
| **Rocks: 1.75 oz** |

### 🥶**Shaking Cocktails**

The quickest way to chill a drink is by using an all-metal Boston shaker, which effectively conducts cold and combines ingredients, especially those with fruit juice or cream.

* **Use an all-metal Boston shaker:** Metal conducts cold better than glass, making it ideal for chilling drinks quickly.  
* **Shake drinks with fruit juice or cream:** Shaking fully combines the ingredients for a well-mixed cocktail.  
* **Shake vigorously for at least eight seconds:** This ensures the drink is properly chilled and develops fine ice shards that help keep it cool.

### 🥄**Stirring Cocktails**

Stirring is ideal for drinks like the classic Martini, which contain no juice, ensuring they are properly chilled and diluted.

* **Use stirring for non-juice drinks:** Stirring is the preferred technique for cocktails like the Martini.  
* **Proper stirring technique:** Place the bar spoon at the bottom of the mixing cup and stir along the outside of the tin.  
* **Stir for twenty seconds with ice:** This ensures the drink is ice-cold and properly diluted.

### 🌀**Straining Cocktails**

After shaking or stirring a drink, it's important to strain it to keep the ice out, with different methods offering varying levels of speed and consistency.

* **Use a strainer for consistency:** Straining with a strainer is fast and ensures consistent results.  
* **Crack the tins for speed:** Cracking the tins to strain is quicker but may be less reliable.  
* **Both methods are effective:** Choose the method that best suits the drink and the situation.

### 🔨**Building Cocktails**

Certain drinks, like the Mojito or a Sex on the Beach, can be made directly in the glass they're served in, known as a "built" drink.

* **Build the drink in the glass:** Start by combining the ingredients directly in the appropriate glass.  
* **Add ice after combining ingredients:** Once the ingredients are in the glass, add ice.  
* **Stir or toss to mix:** Stir or toss the drink back and forth to properly combine the ingredients.

### 🧉**Layering Cocktails**

To layer a drink like a B-52, start by understanding the ingredients. The highest alcohol content, such as Grand Marnier, floats to the top, while the least alcoholic, Kahlua, sinks, leaving Baileys in the middle. Add each ingredient one by one, carefully pouring them down the side using a bar spoon.

## 🥃Spirit Knowledge

---

### 💡**Overview**

Distilled spirits are made from surplus beer (grains) or wine (fruit), heated until the alcohol evaporates, then condensed back into liquid. Our selection includes high-quality benchmarks and inventive smaller producers.

* **Fermentation:** Sugars from fruit, grain, or vegetable are metabolized by yeast to produce alcohol.  
* **Distillation:** Separates and condenses alcohol. A master distiller removes the first (heads) and last (tails) vapors, keeping only the desirable "heart" of the distillate for quality.  
* **Infused vs. Flavored Spirits:** We create our own infused spirits with real fruit and natural ingredients, avoiding artificially flavored and over-sweetened options.

### 🍸**Vodka**

Vodka is a nearly neutral spirit primarily made from grains like rye, wheat, and corn, or potatoes.

Its popularity in the U.S. since the 1970s is due to its affordability, mixability, and effective marketing by brands like Absolut and Grey Goose. Vodka undergoes multiple distillations and filtration to achieve high alcohol purity, though some flavors remain. The quality of distillations is more crucial than their number when evaluating vodka.

### 🍸**Gin**

Gin starts as a nearly neutral grain distillate, then undergoes a secondary distillation with botanicals like juniper, orange peel, orris root, licorice, angelica, and coriander added. There are several styles of gin produced today, the oldest being Genever (from where we get the word gin, an Anglicization of genever).

**Styles of Gin**

* **Genever or Holland Style Gin:** Derived from a flavorful pot-distilled base, lightly flavored with juniper and spices, often malty and slightly sweet.  
* **Old Tom Gin:** A refined, neutral base flavored with botanicals and sweetened.  
* **London Dry Style Gins:** Lighter and crisper, with juniper and citrus flavors dominating.  
* **New World or International Style Gins:** Feature exotic botanicals like cucumber and rose.

### 🍹**Rum**

Rum is a distilled spirit made from sugarcane products like molasses and guarapo (cane juice). Think sugar when you think rum.

Few rules govern rum production; for example, caramel color can be added. Judge each brand individually.

Rum can be un-aged (e.g., Bacardi Superior), aged (e.g., Cruzan Single Barrel), spiced (e.g., Captain Morgan), or flavored (e.g., Malibu). While produced globally, most rum comes from the Caribbean, Central, and South America.

### 🥃**Whiskey // Scotch Whiskey**

Whiskey/Whisky is a broad category of grain spirits, with major types including:

* **Canadian Whisky:** A blended whisky containing at least 50% neutral grain spirits. Additives like maple syrup and caramel are allowed. **Must age 3 years in any barrel**, often used bourbon barrels. Historically rye-based but now mostly corn.

* **Irish Whiskey:** Made in Ireland from malted barley and other grains, triple-distilled. Must age 3+ years, often in bourbon barrels. Mostly blends like Jameson. No smoky peat flavor.

* **Tennessee Whiskey:** Nearly identical to bourbon but undergoes an extra maple charcoal filtering step before aging (Lincoln County Process).

### **SCOTCH WHISKY**

Scotch, considered the epicenter of the whisky world, **is primarily made from malted barley**, though corn and rye are also used, and often aged in ex-bourbon barrels. The main categories are:

* **Single Malt:** 100% malted barley whisky distilled at a single distillery using pot stills. Highly sought-after.  
* **Blended Scotch:** A blend of single malt and grain whiskies from multiple distilleries.

Scotch is found in bars globally and represents some of the most prestigious whisky brands.

### 🍷**BRANDY: DISTILLED WINE**

Brandy is another broad range of spirits that can be made from any fruit, but generally, when you talk about brandy, you're referring to grape brandy. Brandy, in its simplest form, is nothing more than **distilled wine**.

### 🥃**Burbon & Rye**

Bourbon must contain at least 51% corn, be aged minimum 2 years in new charred oak barrels, and cannot be distilled above 160 proof or bottled below 80 proof. The grain bill includes corn, wheat, rye, and barley.

| Wheat-heavy bourbons like Maker's Mark are sweeter. |
| :---: |
| **Rye-forward ones like Buffalo Trace are spicier.** |

### **Rye Whiskey**

Rye whiskey follows the same rules but requires a minimum 51% rye in the mash bill instead of corn. It has a crisp, spicy, peppery flavor profile that was popular pre-Prohibition and is making a comeback, especially in cocktails like the Manhattan and Sazerac.

### 🌵**Tequila**

Tequila is a distilled spirit made from the heart (piña) of large agave plants, which must grow for 5-10 years before being harvested. It is tightly controlled and can only be produced in five Mexican states: Jalisco, Nayarit, Guanajuato, Michoacán, and Tamaulipas. Tequila from the Highlands is citrusy and bright, while Lowland Tequilas are earthy and vegetal. Mezcal, a similar spirit, often has earthy, spicy, and smoky flavors.

### **Categories of Tequila:**

| (1) Mixto: At least 51% agave, blended with neutral grain spirits. (2) Gold: A mixto flavored and colored with caramel. |
| :---- |
| **(3) Blanco/Silver/Platinum: Aged no more than 2 months. (4) Reposado: Aged 2-12 months in any size container. (5) Añejo: Aged 1-3 years in small barrels. (6) Extra Añejo: Aged more than 3 years in small barrels.** |

### 🍇**Liqueurs "Cordials"**

Liqueurs are alcoholic beverages made from a spirit base flavored with fruits, nuts, herbs, spices, etc. Benedictine and Chartreuse are among the oldest continuously produced liqueurs. We offer premium, recognizable brands across categories:

#### **Types of Liqueurs**

* **Fruit/Nut:** Chambord, Grand Marnier, Amaretto, Frangelico  
* **Botanical:** Benedictine, Chartreuse, St. Germain, Crème de Violette  
* **Vermouths/Quinquinas:** Antica Formula, Martini & Rossi, Dolin  
* **Bitter Liqueurs:** Campari, Aperol, Fernet Branca, Averna

Bitters are strong infusions used drop-by-drop to season cocktails, adding depth that's noticeable when missing in a poorly made drink.

## 📚Glossary of Relevant Terms

---

### 🧠**Bartender Terminology**

* **Back:** A non-alcoholic drink served alongside a shot. Backs are also sometimes called chasers. Sometimes a small beer served alongside a shot is considered a back.

* **Bruised:** The drink is shaken vigorously and then strained, resulting in a cloudy look due to ice crystals.

* **Chaser:** See "Back".

* **Dash:** A few drops of an ingredient is considered a dash, usually 1/16 of an ounce or less.

* **Floating:** Adding a layer of liquor to the top of the drink, using a bar spoon for this purpose.

* **Garnish:** Something of an ornamental nature served on the rim of the glass or in the glass, such as olives, pearl onions, slices of fruit, or maraschino cherries.

* **Jigger:** A jigger is used to measure approximately an ounce to an ounce and a half of alcohol.

* **Layered:** A layered drink has the different liquors in layers, usually with the heaviest ones at the bottom, served unmixed.

* **Liqueur:** A sweetened, flavored, spirit-based product such as Frangelicio.

* **Neat:** When a drink is served neat, it is poured into a glass with no ice or mixers.

* **On the rocks:** The proverbial rocks are ice cubes, and a drink served on the rocks is simply a beverage served over a tumbler of ice. Often, the drink is prepared "up" and then strained over the rocks. A "rocks charge" indicates a larger portion.

* **Salt:** Several mixed drinks, such as the margarita, should be served with salt on the rim of the glass. To salt the rim, rub a cut lemon or lime around the rim and then dip the glass into rock or kosher salt.

* **Splash:** A small amount of mixer added to a drink.

* **Spirits:** Distilled alcoholic beverages such as vodka, rum, gin, whiskey...

* **Straight Up:** Can be tricky, could mean "neat" or "up" in a shot glass... Apply common sense here and ask if you're unsure.

* **Tall:** Served in a tall, Collins-style glass.

* **Twist:** A bit of lemon peel that is rubbed on the edge of a glass, sometimes dropped into the drink.

* **Up:** A drink served up should be mixed with ice, for example, in a cocktail shaker, but then strained so there is no ice in the drink when it is served cold.
